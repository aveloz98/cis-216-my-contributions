Manuscript Monsters: Origins of Halloween Iconography

Dr. Jekyll & Mr. Hyde

While they aren't as iconic in current pop culture as a monster such as Dracula, you probably have some idea of who the characters Jekyll & Hyde are. From cartoons to costume stores, references are made often to the general story and idea of a good scientist who transforms into his evil alter ego. What many people don't know, though, is that the characters themselves are originally from a narrative with plenty significant differences from the story we retell today. For example, a fun fact—in the original 1886 novella, Robert Louis Stevenson's Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde , Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde are presented as two completely seperate people, with the fact that they're the same man being a twist toward the end of the story that shocked the readers of the time.
This is the case for nearly every character we think of when we think Halloween. Iconic monsters we dress as or watch onscreen every October all have their origin stories, and many of them were vastly different from how they've grown to be known in popular culture.

  2. Gaston Leroux's The Phantom of the Opera (1910)
